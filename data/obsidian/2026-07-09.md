# 2026-07-09

**SUPREMO: chest-wall RT halved 10yr recurrence (2.5%→1.1%, HR 0.45) with no DFS/OS gain; RNI-prohibited design can't license omitting nodal PMRT.**

> [!summary] TL;DR
> Prostate carried the day's RT de-escalation signal. HYDRA's IPD meta-analysis: dose-escalated moderate hypofractionation adds late G2+ bowel toxicity (OR 1.48) for no PFS gain, reinforcing 60/20; an ASTRO SBRT review pins PACE-B's GU excess on an uncontoured urethra. In breast, SUPREMO's chest-wall RT benefit (HR 0.45) doesn't reach survival or license omitting nodal PMRT.

## 🌰 Prostate

Two RT-technique reads converge on de-escalation: moderate hypofractionation at isodose (60/20) stays standard, and SBRT's GU toxicity tracks urethra dose constraints, not the modality.

### SBRT for Unfavorable Intermediate-Risk Prostate Cancer

> ASTRO 2024 educational round-up: 5-fraction SBRT matches conventional RT efficacy at ~1-2% bothersome toxicity; PACE-B's higher GU signal tied to an uncontoured urethra.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> SBRT's higher GU toxicity looks technique-driven, not intrinsic to ultra-hypofractionation: PACE-B constrained the urethra only 'if visualized', so hotspots likely reached ≥48Gy (~121Gy EQD2). The meta-analysis dose-response (biochemical control P=.018 but late G3+ GU P=.014) argues the fix is contouring and constraining the urethra, not backing off dose.

- 🔍 ASTRO 2024 EDU 16 session (D. Spratt): role of SBRT in unfavorable intermediate-risk (UIR) prostate
- 🔍 UIR (a 2013 prognostic split) carries higher risk: GG3 HR 3.49, ≥2 intermediate-risk factors HR 2.40 for distant mets
- 📊 RTOG 9408 secondary analysis (N=1,068): UIR prognosis and ADT benefit

  | Endpoint | UIR vs FIR (prognosis) | ADT benefit in UIR |
  |---|---|---|
  | Distant metastasis | HR 2.36 (1.44-3.89), P=.001 | HR 0.48 (0.28-0.83), P=.008 |
  | PCSM | HR 1.84 (1.29-2.62), P=.001 | HR 0.40 (0.26-0.60), P<.001 |

- 🔍 Evolution: 45fx/9wk (10-30% bothersome tox) → 5fx SBRT/1-2wk (1-2%)
- 🔍 NCCN lists SBRT for ~all risk groups incl UIR, grounded in HYPO-RT-PC (N=1,200): 42.7Gy/7fx (6.1Gy) vs 78Gy/39fx
- 📊 PACE-B 5yr: no significant EFS difference (trend favoring SBRT)
- 📊 PACE-B G2+ GU 5.4% SBRT vs 3.7% conventional (p=0.28, ns); bowel toxicity not different
- ⚠️ Spratt critique: PACE-B urethra contoured only 'if visualized' → likely hotspots ≥48Gy (~121Gy EQD2), a plausible driver of the GU signal
- 📊 Meta-analysis late G3+: GU 2.0% (1.4-2.8%), GI 1.1% (0.6-2.0%)
- 📐 Dose↑ → better biochemical control (P=.018) but worse late G3+ GU (P=.014); Spratt attributes toxicity to missing dose constraints, not SBRT itself
- 🔍 Three SBRT strategies discussed; field moving toward focal boost + whole-gland de-escalation
  - Traditional: whole prostate to uniform dose
  - Micro-focal boost: full-dose gland + dominant-nodule boost
  - Focal boost + de-escalation: lower whole-gland dose, boosted nodule
- 🔍 Focal-boost SBRT dose data (all 5 fractions)
  - Phase 1a/b: 36.25Gy prostate + nodule boost 45/47.5/50Gy, no G3+ toxicity
  - Loblaw: 35Gy prostate, 25Gy pelvis, 50Gy dominant intraprostatic lesion

### HYDRA

> PFS non-inferior vs CFRT for isodose (HR 0.92) and dose-escalated (HR 0.94) MHFRT; dose escalation adds late G2+ bowel toxicity (OR 1.48).

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> Dose escalation on top of hypofractionation buys no PFS (HR 0.94, ns) yet adds late G2+ GI (OR 1.48) and patient-reported bowel-QoL harm (OR 1.68). The regimen decision lands on isodose 60 Gy/20 fx, not a higher-EQD2 hypofractionated schedule.

- 🔍 IPD meta-analysis (MARCAP), 7 phase 3 RCTs: 3454 pts across 3 isodose-MHFRT trials, 2426 across 4 dose-escalated-MHFRT trials
- 🔍 Median f/u 5.4 yr (isodose), 7.1 yr (dose-escalated); CFRT comparator required ≥70 Gy in 2 Gy-equivalents
- 📐 Isodose MHFRT = same EQD2 as CFRT (eg 60 Gy/20 fx); dose-escalated MHFRT delivers a higher biological dose
- 📊 Both MHFRT schedules PFS-non-inferior to CFRT; dose escalation added no efficacy
- ⚠️ Dose escalation costs late bowel toxicity for nothing: G2+ GI OR 1.48 (p=0.0035), bowel-QoL decrement OR 1.68 (p=0.023), no PFS gain
- ⚠️ Isodose vs dose-escalated is an INDIRECT comparison (separate trial sets, separate CFRT controls), not a randomised head-to-head
- 🔗 Reinforces 60/20 (CHHiP/PROFIT-established) as standard MHFRT; argues against pushing to a higher-EQD2 hypofractionated schedule

**Sources:**
- 📄 [[PMID 40112848]] **Hypofractionated radiotherapy for prostate cancer (HYDRA): an individual patient data meta-analysis of randomised trials in the MARCAP consortium.** — Kishan; Sun; Tree et al.. *The Lancet. Oncology* (2025-04). [doi:10.1016/s1470-2045(25)00034-8](https://doi.org/10.1016/s1470-2045(25%2900034-8)
  > BACKGROUND: Trials comparing moderately hypofractionated radiotherapy (MHFRT) to conventionally-fractionated radiotherapy (CFRT) for prostate cancer have varied considerably in intent (non-inferiority vs superiority) and MHFRT dose. We compare the efficacy and toxicity profiles of isodose MHFRT and dose-escalated MHFRT.
  > METHODS: This was an individual patient data meta-analysis that identified randomised phase 3 trials of CFRT versus MHFRT that had published individual patient-level data on efficacy and late toxicity. A systematic literature search using MEDLINE, Embase, trial registries, the Web of Science, Scopus, and relevant conference proceedings was initially conducted on Dec 15, 2023, and was re-conducted on Jan 8, 2025. Trials that did not publish efficacy data, did not publish late toxicity data, or did not use modern dose radiotherapy (&#x2265;70 Gy in 2 Gy equivalents) in the CFRT group were excluded. Individual patient data were provided to MARCAP by study investigators. Three separate meta-analyses were designed to compare efficacy (primary endpoint was progression-free survival), physician-scored late toxicity (co-primary endpoints were late grade 2 or higher genitourinary and late grade 2 or higher gastrointestinal toxic effects), and patient-reported outcomes (co-primary endpoints were clinically-significant decrements in patient-reported urinary or bowel quality of life) between patients receiving CFRT versus MHFRT.
  > FINDINGS: We identified 1696 records for review. Seven phase 3 trials comparing MHFRT with CFRT were eligible for inclusion in our analysis. Individual patient data were obtained from these seven studies (3454 patients from three trials comparing CFRT with isodose MHFRT and 2426 patients from four trials comparing CFRT with dose-escalated MHFRT). At a median follow-up of 5&#xb7;4 years (IQR 4&#xb7;6-7&#xb7;2) for isodose MHFRT and 7&#xb7;1 years (5&#xb7;7-8&#xb7;4) for dose-escalated MHFRT, no differences in progression-free survival were detected (hazard ratio 0&#xb7;92, 95% CI 0&#xb7;81-1&#xb7;05; p=0&#xb7;21 and 0&#xb7;94, 0&#xb7;82-1&#xb7;09; p=0&#xb7;43 respectively). No increased odds of grade 2 or higher genitourinary toxic effects were identified for either isodose (odds ratio [OR] 1&#xb7;16, 95 CI% 0&#xb7;86-1&#xb7;57; p=0&#xb7;32) or dose-escalated MHFRT (1&#xb7;20, 0&#xb7;95-1&#xb7;51; p=0&#xb7;13). The odds of grade 2 or higher gastrointestinal toxic effects were significantly higher with dose-escalated (OR 1&#xb7;48, 95% CI 1&#xb7;14-1&#xb7;92; p=0&#xb7;0035) but not isodose MHFRT (1&#xb7;30, 0&#xb7;59-2&#xb7;87; p=0&#xb7;51). Isodose MHFRT was not found to show different odds of urinary quality-of-life decrement (OR 1&#xb7;03, 95% CI 0&#xb7;51-2&#xb7;09; p=0&#xb7;93) or bowel quality-of-life decrement (0&#xb7;76, 0&#xb7;40-1&#xb7;43; p=0&#xb7;39). Dose-escalated MHFRT was associated with greater odds of bowel quality-of-life decrement (OR 1&#xb7;68, 95% CI 1&#xb7;07-2&#xb7;61; p=0&#xb7;023), but no evidence of greater urinary quality-of-life decrement was found (1&#xb7;57, 0&#xb7;87-2&#xb7;85; p=0&#xb7;13).
  > INTERPRETATION: Isodose MHFRT and dose-escalated MHFRT both have similar efficacy compared with CFRT, but dose-escalated MHFRT is associated with higher physician-scored and patient-reported bowel toxicity. Isodose regimens, eg, 60 Gy in 20 fractions, should be the standard MHFRT regimen for localised prostate cancer.
  > FUNDING: None.

> [!question] Open questions
> - Isodose vs dose-escalated MHFRT lacks a randomised head-to-head
> - Isodose MHFRT vs ultrahypofractionated SBRT
> - Durability of efficacy and late toxicity beyond 10 years

## 🎀 Breast

### SUPREMO

> Chest-wall RT cut 10yr chest-wall recurrence 2.5%→1.1% (HR 0.45) but no DFS/OS gain; RNI prohibited, so 'omit PMRT' overreads a chest-wall-only trial.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The supplement, not the null headline, carries the RT read: even chest-wall-only RT cut node-positive LRR 4.8%→3.3% (HR 0.51, 0.27-0.96), nothing for node-negative. SUPREMO prohibited RNI and covered SCV in only 12%, so it can't license omitting comprehensive PMRT in 1-3 node-positive disease.

- 📊 SUPREMO outcomes, chest-wall RT vs observation

  | Endpoint | No RT | Chest-wall RT | HR (95% CI) |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | 10yr chest-wall recurrence | 2.5% | 1.1% | 0.45 |
  | LRR, 1-3 nodes (suppl) | 4.8% | 3.3% | 0.51 (0.27-0.96) |

- 🔍 Randomized chest-wall irradiation alone vs observation after upfront mastectomy
- 🔍 Only 25% truly node-negative; majority had N1 (1-3 positive nodes)
- 📐 RNI prohibited; nodal coverage in the PMRT arm was minimal
  - Supraclavicular: only 12% (n=97)
  - Internal mammary: <2% both arms
- ⚠️ Not comprehensive PMRT: no axillary/SCV/IMN coverage, unlike Danish/BC, EORTC-22922, MA-20
- 🔗 EBCTCG 2014 meta-analysis, PMRT benefit by nodal status
  - 1-3 positive nodes: 10yr LRR cut 17.9%, 20yr breast-cancer mortality cut 8%
  - Node-negative: no significant PMRT benefit
- ⚠️ TNBC subgroup (only 10%) worse with PMRT on forest plot, likely underpowering not harm
- 🔗 Chinese TNBC RCT (n=681): chest-wall PMRT improved 5yr recurrence and OS, opposite here
- ⚠️ 65% HR+ but only 10yr follow-up; late recurrences likely underascertained
