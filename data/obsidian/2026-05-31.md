# 2026-05-31

**CAN-2409 + definitive 78Gy RT: DFS HR 0.7 (p=0.0155), 2yr biopsy pCR 80 vs 64%; a local-control gain without dose escalation or ADT, OS/PCSM still immature.**

> [!summary] TL;DR
> Prostate carried the RT news, and the recurring lesson is that intensifying on top of definitive RT mostly fails hard endpoints: ENZARAD (enza add-on) and COMPPARE (proton vs IMRT) both null, while CAN-2409 bought local control but not yet OS. Outside RT, RASolute 302 hit mOS 13.2 vs 6.6mo (HR 0.40) in 2L pancreatic.

## 🌰 Prostate

Four prostate reads probing intensification on definitive RT: enzalutamide and protons added nothing on hard endpoints, an oncolytic virus moved only local control, and PROTEUS stays an unreadable preview.

### COMPPARE

> No proton advantage over IMRT: bowel urgency 5.7 vs 6%, GI G2+ tox 5.2 vs 5.6%, 3yr biochemical control 98.0 vs 97.9%, all NS.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> Not proton vs photon but the rectal spacer is the toxicity lever: it cut 2yr GI G2+ toxicity (4.4% IMRT, 4.7% proton with spacer vs 7.2% and 8.7% without, p=0.009), dwarfing modality. Both arms fell far below hypothesized rates, so paying the proton premium for less GI toxicity isn't supported.

![COMPPARE](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJrbdsMW8AYkCpV.jpg)

| Endpoint | IMRT | Proton | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bowel urgency (EPIC) | 6% | 5.7% | 0.28 |
| Bowel frequency (EPIC) | 4% | 3.5% | 0.43 |
| GI tox (CTCAE ≥G2) | 5.6% | 5.2% | 0.60 |
| 3yr freedom from progression | 97.9% | 98.0% | 0.90 |

![COMPPARE](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJrbduxXoAMiv-c.jpg)

| Group | 2yr GI G2+ toxicity (95% CI) |
|---|---|
| IMRT, no spacer | 7.2% (5.0%, 9.9%) |
| Proton, no spacer | 8.7% (5.0%, 14%) |
| IMRT, spacer | 4.4% (2.8%, 6.4%) |
| Proton, spacer | 4.7% (3.6%, 6.0%) |

- 🔍 Prospective comparative-effectiveness cohort (non-randomized), PCORI-funded; 51 centers, 2524 accrued Jul 2018–Oct 2022
- 🔍 Cohorts: 1500 proton vs 1000 photon; all de novo localized prostate excl very-high-risk and metastatic
- ⚠️ Realized GI/bowel toxicity ~5-6% in both arms, far below the 15-29% hypothesized for IMRT
- ⚠️ Low realized event rates leave the comparison likely underpowered to detect a true proton benefit
- 📊 Rectal spacer cut 2yr GI G2+ toxicity more than modality choice (p=0.009); proton vs IMRT negligible within spacer strata
- 🔗 vs PARTIQoL (randomized proton vs IMRT, localized prostate): concordant null for patient-reported bowel/GI endpoints
- ⚠️ Early results; late GI/GU toxicity (the main proton rationale) and long-term control not yet mature
- ⚠️ Non-randomized cohorts: baseline differences between proton and IMRT pts may confound the read

### CAN-2409

> DFS HR 0.7 (0.52-0.94, p=0.0155), median NR vs 86.1mo, adding intra-prostatic oncolytic virus to definitive 78Gy RT; OS/PCSM immature.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The RT-relevant read is local control: 2yr positive-biopsy fell to 19.6% vs 36.4% (p=0.0015) without dose escalation or added ADT, a new local-intensification lever. But the control RT (78Gy, no boost or ADT) predates modern intensification, leaving its place vs brachy boost or ADT unsettled.

## 🌀 GI Lower

Upfront RT folded into 1L systemic + PD1 for unresectable MSS met rectal ca, but the single-arm design leaves RT's contribution unmeasurable.

### MIRACLE-2

> ORR 68%, mOS 23.2mo, 18% reached NED with upfront RT then systemic + PD1 in 1L unresectable MSS met rectal ca.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The RT read is upfront priming: HFRT to primary plus HFRT/SBRT to mets precedes systemic + PD1, and 18% (9/50) of unresectable pts reached NED, an unusual signal in immune-cold MSS. RT dose/fractionation isn't in source and no omission arm isolates its effect, so adding RT to a systemic backbone stays hypothesis-only.

![MIRACLE-2](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJpxlR1X0AMyvn4.jpg)

- 📊 Efficacy outcomes (single-arm, N=50)

  | Endpoint | Value | 95% CI |
  |---|---|---|
  | ETS rate (1°) | 76.0% | 62.4-86.8% |
  | ORR | 68.0% | 53.6-80.0% |
  | DCR | 88.0% | 76.0-95.2% |
  | Median PFS | 9.3 mo | 7.1-11.5 |
  | Median OS | 23.2 mo | 15.1-31.3 |
  | Median DOR | 8.0 mo | 5.2-10.8 |

- 📊 18% (9/50) reached NED; 1-yr OS 93.3%, 1-yr PFS 33.4%, 1-yr DOR 20%
- 🔍 Prospective single-arm phase I, N=50, 1L MSS unresectable met rectal ca, primary ≤10cm from anal verge
- 🔍 RT given upfront: HFRT to primary, HFRT or SBRT to mets. Dose/fractionation not reported in source
- 💊 Then FOLFOX-bev-tislelizumab (RAS/BRAF-mut) or FOLFIRI-cetux-tislelizumab (WT); RAS/BRAF mut in 56.0%
- ⚠️ Grade 3/4 TRAEs, heme-dominant
  - Lymphopenia 36.7%
  - Neutropenia 26.5%
  - Leukopenia 20.4%
- ⚠️ RT-associated lymphopenia a plausible driver of the 36.7% G3/4 lymphopenia
- ⚠️ RT contribution unmeasurable: no RT-omission arm, effect confounded with systemic + PD1
- ⚠️ 1° EP is ETS (≥20% shrinkage at 8wk), an early surrogate not OS/PFS; mFU only 19.9mo (16.4-23.4)
- ⚠️ Durability thin: 1-yr DOR 20%, mDOR 8.0mo despite high 1-yr OS
- 🔗 MSS ~95% of mCRC and intrinsically IO-resistant; single-agent PD1 inactive in MSS

- 🔍 Randomized 2L metastatic PDAC: daraxonrasib vs investigator's-choice chemotherapy

## 🫁 Thoracic / Lung

### OCEANUS

> Sequential iRT mOS 20.3 vs 16.0mo, HR 0.68 (P=.045) vs concurrent in newly-dx advanced NSCLC; real-world Hong Kong cohort.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> Timing is the read: sequential iRT showed longer OS than concurrent (mOS 20.3 vs 16.0mo, HR 0.68) in newly-dx advanced NSCLC. RT dose, fractionation, and target volume go unreported, so it can't say which regimen earned the gap. Moves the concurrent-vs-sequential timing question only, in stage IV.

- 🔍 Real-world Hong Kong CDARS (>90% population coverage); NSCLC dx 2010-2021, propensity overlap-weighted, IPTW sensitivity
- 🔍 335 of 3522 ICI-treated pts received RT: 155 newly-dx advanced, 180 refractory; 73.7% male, median age 64
- 📊 Newly-dx advanced NSCLC: sequential vs concurrent iRT (real-world OS)

  | Endpoint | Sequential iRT | Concurrent iRT | Adjusted HR / P |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Median OS (95% CI) | 20.3 mo (13.3-NR) | 16.0 mo (8.3-30.0) | 0.68 (0.47-0.99), P=.045 |

- 📊 Refractory NSCLC: RT with vs without ICI maintenance (real-world OS)

  | Endpoint | RT + ICI maintenance | RT without maintenance | P |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Median OS (95% CI) | 11.2 mo (7.9-20.6) | 6.7 mo (4.4-17.4) | P=.20 |

- 📊 Chemotherapy associated with longer OS in newly-dx advanced; not significant in refractory
- 📐 Proportional hazards violated (Schoenfeld); treatment effects also summarized by RMST
- ⚠️ Sequencing not randomized: confounding by indication likely (concurrent may mark more urgent/bulky disease)
- ⚠️ RT dose, fractionation, technique, and target volume not reported in source, limits transfer to practice
- ⚠️ Refractory analysis required ≥90-day survival (landmark); small per-arm N, wide CIs; authors call it hypothesis-generating

**Sources:**
- 📄 [[PMID 41885834]] **Combination of Radiotherapy and Immunotherapy in Advanced Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer.** — Zhou; Wang; Lee et al.. *JAMA oncology* (2026-05). [doi:10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.0392](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.0392)
  > IMPORTANCE: The optimal sequencing of radiotherapy (RT) combined with immunotherapy (iRT) and the value of chemotherapy remain undefined for advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), where randomized data are limited.
  > OBJECTIVE: To compare real-world overall survival (OS) between sequential and concurrent iRT in newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC, assess the effect of immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) maintenance after RT in refractory disease, and evaluate the association of chemotherapy with survival.
  > DESIGN, SETTING, AND PARTICIPANTS: This is a territory-wide study (OCEANUS) based on the Hong Kong Hospital Authority Clinical Data Analysis and Reporting System (more than 90% population coverage). Patients with NSCLC diagnosed from January 1, 2010, to December 31, 2021, who subsequently received iRT for advanced or refractory disease were included. Overlap weighting was the primary propensity score-weighted method, with inverse probability of treatment weighting used for sensitivity analysis. Data were analyzed from December 2024 to April 2025.
  > EXPOSURES: Sequential vs concurrent iRT for newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC; RT with vs without ICI maintenance for refractory NSCLC; receipt of chemotherapy.
  > MAIN OUTCOMES AND MEASURES: The primary outcome was real-world OS after landmark, estimated with weighted Kaplan-Meier and Cox models. When proportional hazards were violated (per Schoenfeld residuals), treatment effects were summarized using restricted mean survival time.
  > RESULTS: Of 3522 patients who received ICIs, 335 received RT, including 155 with newly diagnosed advanced and 180 with refractory NSCLC. Of these, 247 (73.7%) were male, and the median (range) age was 64 (34-90) years. In newly diagnosed NSCLC, patients treated with sequential iRT had significant longer real-world OS than those treated with concurrent iRT (median, 20.3 months [95% CI, 13.3 to not reached] vs 16.0 months [95% CI, 8.3-30.0]; adjusted hazard ratio, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.47-0.99; P&#x2009;=&#x2009;.045). Chemotherapy was also associated with longer survival in patients with newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC. In refractory NSCLC, RT with ICI maintenance was associated with a numerically longer median real-world OS (11.2 months [95% CI, 7.9-20.6] vs 6.7 months [95% CI, 4.4-17.4]; P&#x2009;=&#x2009;.20). Addition of chemotherapy was not significant for real-world OS. Inverse probability of treatment weighting analyses produced similar estimates.
  > CONCLUSIONS AND RELEVANCE: In this cohort study, sequential iRT was associated with longer survival than concurrent iRT in patients with newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC, and chemotherapy was associated with longer survival. In patients with refractory NSCLC who survived at least 90 days, RT with ICI maintenance resulted in nonsignificantly longer survival and an unclear association with chemotherapy. These findings are hypothesis generating and support prospective randomized studies to define optimal sequencing of iRT and use of systemic treatment partners.
