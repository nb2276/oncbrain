# 2026-05-31

**RASolute 302: mOS 13.2 vs 6.7mo (HR 0.40), daraxonrasib roughly doubles OS in previously-treated metastatic pancreatic ca.**

> [!summary] TL;DR
> GI posted the day's biggest number (RASolute 302 roughly doubling 2L pancreatic OS), but the RT slate is the reader's story: in prostate, adding enza (ENZARAD) or protons (COMPPARE) to the RT+ADT backbone failed while intraprostatic CAN-2409 improved DFS (HR 0.70); thoracic OCEANUS found sequential iRT beat concurrent (HR 0.68, real-world).

## 🌰 Prostate

RT-intensification theme dominates: adding enza (ENZARAD) or protons (COMPPARE) to the RT+ADT backbone failed, while intraprostatic CAN-2409 hit its DFS endpoint.

### COMPPARE

> Proton vs IMRT: no difference in bowel urgency (6% vs 5.7%), ≥G2 GI tox, or 3-yr biochemical control in localized prostate.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The RT read is that rectal spacer use, not proton vs photon, moved GI toxicity: 2-yr G2+ fell to ~4.4-4.7% with a spacer vs 7.2-8.7% without (p=0.009), similar across modalities. Protons showed no toxicity or 3-yr biochemical-control edge, arguing against the proton premium for localized disease.

- 🔍 Prospective comparative effectiveness (COMPPARE, PCORI-funded); non-randomized proton vs photon cohorts, 51 centers, 2524 accrued 2018-2022
- 🔍 De novo localized prostate, excl very-high-risk + metastatic; proton n=1500, photon n=1000
- 📊 No powered endpoint favored protons; observed rates near-identical, all p≥0.28
- 🔍 Observed GI tox far below design assumptions (hypothesized IMRT 29%, actual 5.6%), reflecting modern technique + spacers
- ⚠️ Non-randomized cohorts: selection bias and residual confounding limit the proton-vs-IMRT comparison
- ⚠️ Early results, short f/u; late GU/GI toxicity (protons' theoretical edge) and long-term control unassessed
- 🔗 Directionally consistent with PARTIQoL (randomized proton vs IMRT, localized prostate): no bowel-QoL advantage for protons

### CAN-2409 · [[NCT01436968]]

> DFS HR 0.70 (0.52-0.94, p=0.016) adding intraprostatic CAN-2409 to EBRT in intermediate/high-risk localised prostate; OS and PCSM immature.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The RT read is local control: 2yr biopsy positivity fell 36.4% to 19.6% (p=0.0015), comparable to adding ADT (RTOG 9408) but on standard 78Gy without a brachy or SIB boost. Whether it adds over modern dose-intensification plus ADT is the open integration question, since ADT was optional and no boost was used.

![CAN-2409](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/Gshzd0nWAAAgwmm.jpg)

|---|---|---|---|
| DFS median | NR | 86.1 mo | HR 0.7 (0.52-0.94), p=0.0155 |
| 2yr biopsy pCR | 80.4% | 63.6% | n/a |
| 2yr local recurrence | 19.6% | 36.4% | p=0.0015 |

![CAN-2409](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/Gshz8TuWgAENQ46.jpg)

| Trial | Intensifier | Local endpoint: control → intensified |
|---|---|---|
| RTOG 9408 | +4mo ADT | 2yr biopsy+ 40% → 20% |
| ASCENDE-RT | LDR brachy boost | 10y local failure 7.1% → 1.5% |
| FLAME | SIB 95Gy | Crude local failure 7.7% → 2.7% |
| CAN-2409 | +CAN-2409 | 2yr biopsy+ 36.4% → 19.6% |

- 🔍 Phase 3 double-blind placebo-controlled RCT, 2:1 randomisation, N=745, 51 US/Puerto Rico centres
- 🔍 Primary EP disease-free survival (ITT); accrued 2012-2021, median follow-up 50.3mo
- 🔍 RT: EBRT 78Gy/2Gy or hypofx (60Gy/3Gy, 70Gy/2.5Gy); ADT optional
- 💊 3 intraprostatic aglatimagene injections (5×10^11 viral particles) + valacyclovir vs placebo + valacyclovir
- ⚠️ DFS is a composite (recurrence or death); OS and PCSM immature, 1 PCSM event per arm
- ⚠️ ADT optional, no brachy/SIB boost mandated; added value over modern dose intensification unresolved
- ⚠️ Accrual 2012-2021 predates PSMA staging; 'high-risk' cohort likely differs from PSMA-staged pts today
- 📊 Grade 3+ safety comparable; no treatment-related deaths

- 📄 **Aglatimagene besadenovec (CAN-2409) with radiotherapy for patients with localised prostate cancer: a phase 3, multicentre, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial** — DeWeese, Theodore L; Manzanera, Andrea; Sylvester, John et al.. *The Lancet Oncology* (2026-06). [doi:10.1016/s1470-2045(26)00071-9](https://doi.org/10.1016/s1470-2045(26%2900071-9)
  > Background About 30% of men with localised prostate cancer undergoing radiotherapy with curative intent have disease recurrence associated with progression-related symptoms and substantial toxicity of salvage therapies. Previous studies with aglatimagene besadenovec (CAN-2409, hereafter referred to as aglatimagene) showed synergy with radiation and immune-mediated cytotoxicity in patients with prostate cancer. We aimed to assess whether addition of aglatimagene plus prodrug (valacyclovir) to standard-of-care external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) could improve disease-free survival in this population. Methods We conducted a phase 3, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial at 51 medical centres (26 community and 25 institutional or military) across the USA and Puerto Rico in patients with intermediate or high-risk prostate cancer. Patients aged at least 18 years who were planning to undergo EBRT and with an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group score of 0-2 were eligible. Patients were randomly assigned (2:1) via central block-randomisation to receive either three courses of intraprostatic aglatimagene (5 × 10 11 viral particles) plus valacyclovir or placebo plus valacyclovir, with randomisation stratified by risk category and androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) use. Patients received standard-of-care EBRT (78 Gy in 2 Gy fractions) or hypofractionated EBRT (60 Gy in 3 Gy fractions or 70 Gy in 2·5 Gy fractions) with optional ADT. The primary endpoint was disease-free survival, defined as time-from-randomisation to prostate cancer recurrence or death in the intent-to-treat population (all randomly assigned patients). Safety was assessed in all individuals who received at least one injection. The trial is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov, [[NCT01436968]], and long-term follow-up is ongoing. Findings Between Feb 21, 2012, and Sept 9, 2021, 745 men (591 [79%] White, 121 [16%] Black) were randomly assigned to receive aglatimagene plus valacyclovir (n=496) or placebo plus valacyclovir (n=249). After a median follow-up of 50·3 months (IQR 35·2-63·3), median disease-free survival was not reached (95% CI 121·78 to not reached) in the aglatimagene plus valacyclovir group versus 86·1 (IQR 29·7-143·0) months in the placebo plus valacyclovir group (hazard ratio 0·70, 95% CI 0·52-0·94; p=0·016). Treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) of grade 3 or worse occurred in 40 (8%) of 479 patients in the aglatimagene group and 17 (7%)of 232 patients in the placebo group. The most common TEAE of grade 3 or worse was acute kidney injury in both the aglatimagene group (nine [2%] of 479 patients) and the placebo group (four [2%] of 232 patients). Serious adverse events occurred in 28 (6%) of 479 patients in the aglatimagene group and 17 (7%) of 232 in the placebo group. Treatment-related serious adverse events occurred in eight (2%) patients in the aglatimagene group (four acute kidney injury, two pyrexia, and one each influenza-like symptoms and urinary retention) and five (2%) in the placebo group (four acute kidney injury, and one each increased creatinine levels and skin rash; one patient reported two serious adverse events). No treatment-related deaths were reported. Interpretation Aglatimagene plus valacyclovir was associated with longer disease-free survival than placebo plus valacyclovir when added to standard of radiotherapy for the treatment of localised prostate cancer, offering a meaningful benefit without increasing clinically significant toxicity. Funding Candel Therapeutics and US National Institutes of Health.

### RASolute 302

> mOS 13.2 vs 6.7mo, HR 0.40 (0.30-0.53), P<0.001 for daraxonrasib vs investigator's-choice chemo in previously treated metastatic pancreatic cancer.

|---|---|---|---|

| G12 · Chemo | 6.6 mo (5.4-8.2) | — | 8.7% |

| Overall · Chemo | 6.7 mo (5.8-8.0) | — | 17.3% |

- 🔍 Phase 3 RCT: daraxonrasib (oral RAS(ON) multi-selective inhibitor) vs investigator's-choice chemo in previously treated metastatic pancreatic cancer
- 🔍 Two prespecified populations: RAS G12-mutant (n=228 vs 231) and overall (n=248 vs 252; includes G12/G13/Q61 + no RAS mutation identified)
- 📊 Death events 32% (daraxonrasib) vs 55-56% (chemo), both populations; medians reached
- 💊 Single oral targeted agent standing in for cytotoxic chemo at 2L; targets active GTP-bound RAS across G12 variants
- 🔗 Chemo control (6.6-6.7mo) matches the historic 2L metastatic PDAC benchmark; experimental arm 13.2mo roughly doubles it
- ⚠️ Data from a single ASCO LBA OS figure; full efficacy, safety, and PFS not yet published (abstract 'soon')
- ⚠️ Open-label (oral drug vs IV chemo); OS is a hard endpoint so bias is limited, but any PRO/PFS read needs blinded review
- ⚠️ Daraxonrasib median OS upper CI not reached (10.0-NR); tail durability still immature

### MIRACLE-2

> 68% ORR, mOS 23.2mo, 18% NED with RT-primed chemo + tislelizumab in MSS unresectable met rectal ca.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> The RT-relevant read is technique: HFRT to the primary plus HFRT/SBRT to mets, delivered first to prime immunity, but no dose or fractionation in source, so it doesn't transfer to practice yet. 18% (9/50) converted to NED via resection or watch-and-wait. Near-universal lymphopenia (95.9% all-grade, 36.7% G3/4) undercuts a strategy premised on RT-driven T-cell activation.

## 🫁 Thoracic / Lung

Real-world only: sequencing RT before ICI beat concurrent iRT for OS, but hypothesis-generating with no RT technique reported.

### OCEANUS

> Sequential iRT beat concurrent for real-world OS in newly-dx advanced NSCLC: median 20.3 vs 16.0 mo, HR 0.68 (0.47-0.99), P=.045.

> [!info] Why it matters · Radiation oncology
> Sequential iRT (ICI and RT not overlapping) beat concurrent for real-world OS in newly-dx advanced NSCLC, HR 0.68 (0.47-0.99), P=.045, favoring temporal separation of RT from ICI. RT dose, fractionation, and target volume aren't in the source, so the signal can't yet transfer to a specific plan. The refractory RT+ICI-maintenance read was NS (P=.20).

- 📊 Real-world OS by iRT comparison (propensity-weighted, landmark)

  | Setting / comparison | Experimental OS | Control OS | HR / P value |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Newly-dx advanced, sequential vs concurrent iRT | 20.3 mo (95% CI 13.3-NR) | 16.0 mo (95% CI 8.3-30.0) | HR 0.68 (0.47-0.99), P=.045 |
  | Refractory, RT + ICI maintenance vs RT alone | 11.2 mo (95% CI 7.9-20.6) | 6.7 mo (95% CI 4.4-17.4) | P=.20 (ns) |

- 🔍 Territory-wide real-world cohort (Hong Kong CDARS, >90% population coverage); NSCLC dx 2010-2021 who received iRT
- 🔍 335 of 3522 ICI-treated pts got RT: 155 newly-dx advanced, 180 refractory. Median age 64 (34-90), 73.7% male
- 🔍 Overlap-weighting propensity score primary; IPTW sensitivity gave similar estimates
- 💊 Chemotherapy associated with longer OS in newly-dx; not significant in refractory
- ⚠️ Observational: sequential vs concurrent not randomized, confounding by indication plausible despite weighting
- ⚠️ No RT dose, fractionation, modality, or target volume reported in source; authors call findings hypothesis-generating
- 🔗 Direction (RT before ICI favored) aligns with PACIFIC paradigm, though PACIFIC was curative-intent stage III chemoRT, not advanced disease

**Sources:**
- 📄 [[PMID 41885834]] **Combination of Radiotherapy and Immunotherapy in Advanced Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer.** — Zhou; Wang; Lee et al.. *JAMA oncology* (2026-05). [doi:10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.0392](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.0392)
  > IMPORTANCE: The optimal sequencing of radiotherapy (RT) combined with immunotherapy (iRT) and the value of chemotherapy remain undefined for advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), where randomized data are limited.
  > OBJECTIVE: To compare real-world overall survival (OS) between sequential and concurrent iRT in newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC, assess the effect of immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) maintenance after RT in refractory disease, and evaluate the association of chemotherapy with survival.
  > DESIGN, SETTING, AND PARTICIPANTS: This is a territory-wide study (OCEANUS) based on the Hong Kong Hospital Authority Clinical Data Analysis and Reporting System (more than 90% population coverage). Patients with NSCLC diagnosed from January 1, 2010, to December 31, 2021, who subsequently received iRT for advanced or refractory disease were included. Overlap weighting was the primary propensity score-weighted method, with inverse probability of treatment weighting used for sensitivity analysis. Data were analyzed from December 2024 to April 2025.
  > EXPOSURES: Sequential vs concurrent iRT for newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC; RT with vs without ICI maintenance for refractory NSCLC; receipt of chemotherapy.
  > MAIN OUTCOMES AND MEASURES: The primary outcome was real-world OS after landmark, estimated with weighted Kaplan-Meier and Cox models. When proportional hazards were violated (per Schoenfeld residuals), treatment effects were summarized using restricted mean survival time.
  > RESULTS: Of 3522 patients who received ICIs, 335 received RT, including 155 with newly diagnosed advanced and 180 with refractory NSCLC. Of these, 247 (73.7%) were male, and the median (range) age was 64 (34-90) years. In newly diagnosed NSCLC, patients treated with sequential iRT had significant longer real-world OS than those treated with concurrent iRT (median, 20.3 months [95% CI, 13.3 to not reached] vs 16.0 months [95% CI, 8.3-30.0]; adjusted hazard ratio, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.47-0.99; P&#x2009;=&#x2009;.045). Chemotherapy was also associated with longer survival in patients with newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC. In refractory NSCLC, RT with ICI maintenance was associated with a numerically longer median real-world OS (11.2 months [95% CI, 7.9-20.6] vs 6.7 months [95% CI, 4.4-17.4]; P&#x2009;=&#x2009;.20). Addition of chemotherapy was not significant for real-world OS. Inverse probability of treatment weighting analyses produced similar estimates.
  > CONCLUSIONS AND RELEVANCE: In this cohort study, sequential iRT was associated with longer survival than concurrent iRT in patients with newly diagnosed advanced NSCLC, and chemotherapy was associated with longer survival. In patients with refractory NSCLC who survived at least 90 days, RT with ICI maintenance resulted in nonsignificantly longer survival and an unclear association with chemotherapy. These findings are hypothesis generating and support prospective randomized studies to define optimal sequencing of iRT and use of systemic treatment partners.
