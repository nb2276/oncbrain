═══ SIGNIFICANCE (why it matters, for the perspective reader) ═══

Emit a `significance` string: the ONE long-form prose field on the card. Where
the bullets are a terse scan, this is 2 to 3 sentences (aim ≤ 55 words, hard
ceiling 70) that answer "why does this study matter, and what should I do with
it?" for the reader of the SPECIALTY PERSPECTIVE above. If no perspective is
set, write it for a general oncology subspecialist. Short is the point: if two
strong sentences carry the additive fact and the decision, stop there.

This is the place to surface the SUBTLE, ADDITIVE detail that a busy reader
would miss skimming the bullets, the kind of thing that lives in a table cell, a
forest-plot row, a KM subgroup, or a figure's small print:
- the subgroup or secondary signal that changes the read (e.g. a node-positive
  in-field control benefit under an OS-negative headline);
- the technique / dose / fractionation / target-volume / sequencing parameter
  that gates whether the result transfers to THIS reader's practice;
- the local-regional / pattern-of-failure endpoint behind a systemic primary;
- the comparator or magnitude that reframes the bullets' headline;
- the single decision the study moves for the perspective reader (omit vs add,
  escalate vs de-escalate, cover nodes or not, hypofractionate, sequence).

Hard rules for `significance`:
- GROUNDED. Every number is verbatim from the tweets / images / OCR, same bar as
  the bullets. The no-fabrication rule OUTRANKS the lens. If the additive detail
  is a magnitude locked in a figure or supplement NOT in the source text, say so
  ("subgroup HR not reported in source text"); never substitute a guessed number.
- VOICE. Peer-review register, no em dashes (use commas, parentheses, or ·), none
  of the banned vocabulary, no promotional tone. Prose, but tight: no throat-
  clearing ("This study is important because"), no restating the trial name.
- ADDITIVE, not a recap. Do not just re-narrate the TL;DR and bullets in
  paragraph form. If you have nothing beyond the bullets, that is the abstain
  signal.
- LEAD WITH THE SUBSTANCE. Open on the additive fact (the subgroup number, the
  dose/parameter, the decision), not a preamble. A one-clause framing is fine
  ("The RT-relevant read is local control:"); a whole preamble sentence ("For
  an RT reader, this study matters because…") is wasted words.
- DON'T RESTATE THE VERDICT. The verdict pill and its rationale already carry
  the maturity / design caveat (single-arm, small N, surrogate endpoint,
  post-hoc). Do not spend a sentence repeating it. If a limitation earns a word,
  fold it into the clause about the SPECIFIC decision it gates, not its own
  closing sentence.
- When RT (or the active specialty) is not in play and there is no specialty-
  specific "so what," ABSTAIN: set `significance` to null. A forced angle is
  worse than none.

═══ MONDAY CLINIC (the one decision sentence a peer forwards) ═══

of the reader tomorrow whom this study moves, and, where it sharpens the read,
the patient it does NOT. This is the sentence a subspecialist screenshots to a
colleague. Frame it as EVIDENCE CONTEXT, never a directive: name the population,
biomarker, stage, or line of therapy the result applies to and the decision it

Shape: "In [specific population / biomarker / line], this [supports / questions /
does not change] [the decision]; it does not extend to [the population it leaves
untouched]." One sentence, tight, no preamble.

Hard rules for `monday_clinic`:
- DISTINCT FROM significance, the verdict rationale, AND the Limitations section.
  These three fold surfaces sit side by side and MUST each cover a different
  angle: the verdict `rationale` = WHY the verdict (design/maturity caveat),
  the `Limitations` section = the methodological weaknesses, `monday_clinic` =
  WHICH PATIENT it moves. `significance` names what is ADDITIVE to the literature
  (the subgroup, the parameter, the figure signal). If the honest clinic sentence
  would just restate `significance`, the verdict `rationale`, OR the `Limitations`
  body, ABSTAIN: set `monday_clinic` to null. Never pad the card with two prose
  lines that say the same thing.
- GROUNDED. Same no-fabrication bar as significance and the bullets. Never invent
  an eligibility criterion, a subgroup, or a population the source did not state.
- NOT CLINICAL ADVICE. Evidence-context framing only (population / biomarker /
  line / decision). No imperative ("start", "add", "give"), no dose, no
  "recommended". It must read as a peer summarizing evidence, not prescribing.
- ABSTAIN when there is no single clinic-actionable read: a negative trial that
  changes nothing, a guideline / review with no one-patient decision, or a signal
  too immature to move a patient. A forced clinic sentence is worse than none.

═══ PRIMARY ENDPOINT (structured, for the card head) ═══

Emit a `primary_endpoint` object when — and ONLY when — the source STATES a primary
endpoint (i.e. a registered randomized trial). It lets the card lead with the
endpoint TYPE beside the number, because knowing the benefit is on overall survival
vs a surrogate (PFS/MFS/DFS) vs local control matters as much as the HR itself.

Fields:
- `name`: the endpoint, e.g. "Overall survival", "Metastasis-free survival".
- `klass`: one of `overall-survival` | `surrogate` | `local-control` | `safety`.
  OS → `overall-survival`; PFS/MFS/DFS/RFS/bPFS/bFFS → `surrogate`;
  LC/LRR/LRF/local recurrence → `local-control`; an AE/toxicity primary → `safety`.
- `stat_value`: the HEADLINE effect ONLY, verbatim — e.g. "HR 0.62", "82% vs 69%",
  "6.7 vs 13.4 mo". ≤60 chars. This is set big on the card.
- `stat_detail`: the supporting CI / p-value / absolute, verbatim — e.g.
  "95% CI 0.48-0.79, p<0.001" or "ns, P=.34". Carries significance. ≤90 chars, or null.

Hard rules for `primary_endpoint`:
- GROUNDED. Every number in `stat_value` and `stat_detail` is verbatim from
  source; a stated effect-size number not present in the source drops the whole
  field at build. Never invent a number, an endpoint, or a p-value.
- ABSTAIN → null for reviews, single-arm / observational studies, meta-analyses
  with no single stated primary, or abstract-only sources with no primary endpoint.
  A guessed primary is worse than none.
- Report HONESTLY: if the primary was NOT met, `stat_detail` says so (e.g.
  "HR 1.55, ns" / "did not meet OS"). Never cherry-pick a positive secondary.

Rules:
- `body` is 1-3 tight sentences of prose (NOT a leading-emoji bullet). Put the

- Use **markdown bold** for the decision-carrying terms + numbers: primary/secondary
  labels ("**Primary:**"), the eligibility gate, the key effect sizes, the
  verdict-moving phrase (e.g. "**no extra benefit**"). Bold sparingly, not whole lines.
- Split what the emoji bullets lump together: trial DESIGN (phase/N/sites/follow-up)
  vs POPULATION & inclusion (eligibility, baseline) vs REGIMEN (drug/dose/schedule)
  vs RADIOTHERAPY (dose/fractionation/target) are DISTINCT sections when the source
  gives them.
- "Applies to" MUST be exactly two sentences: "Represents [population]. Does not
  represent [excluded / boundary population]." (the card renders these as ✓ / ✕.)
- "Results" holds the effect sizes. If the results are a 2D arm-vs-arm comparison,
  emit them as a `{text, table}` DETAIL (endpoint col 1, intervention arm col 2,
  control col 3, effect size last) and do NOT restate those numbers in the Results
  section body — use the body only for a one-line note, or omit the Results section.
  "Safety" = the AE profile; "Discussion" = the longer interpretive read.
- A `Limitations` section must ADD to the verdict `rationale`, not echo it. The
  rationale already carries the headline caveat (single-arm, small N, surrogate
  endpoint, post-hoc); a Limitations section earns its place only with the NEXT
  layer (comparator vintage, unblinded QoL, subgroup fragility, missing follow-up).
  If the only limitation is the one the rationale already states, OMIT the
  Limitations section — do not repeat it.
- Omit `analysis_sections` (or emit []) for a review / thin abstract with no
  structured depth — the card then falls back to the terse bullets.

- Judge NEUTRALLY across all three subspecialties. The analysis perspective above (if
  one is set) frames the significance PROSE, not this field — do not over-weight your

  specialty gets nothing additive beyond the shared bullets.
- 2 to 3 sentences each, ≤55 words, no em dashes, no banned vocab.
